# Golden Answer — SOTP, Diversified Industrial Conglomerate

Illustrative net debt = $2,500M and diluted shares = 300M (real
submission uses SMB values). Activist claim: conglomerate discount
exists; test with segment-by-segment value.

## 1. Segment Valuations

| Segment | EBITDA ($M) | Peer EV/EBITDA | Low EV ($M) | Mid EV ($M) | High EV ($M) |
|---|---:|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Aerospace & Defense | 440 | 14-18x | 6,160 | 7,040 | 7,920 |
| Industrial Automation | 300 | 16-22x | 4,800 | 5,700 | 6,600 |
| Building Products | 135 | 10-14x | 1,350 | 1,620 | 1,890 |
| Specialty Chemicals | 90 | 8-12x | 720 | 900 | 1,080 |
| **Total Segment EV** | **965** | — | **13,030** | **15,260** | **17,490** |

Peer-set rationale:
- A&D: pure-play aftermarket / MRO peers (e.g., commercial aerospace
  suppliers) — multiples reflect aftermarket stickiness.
- Automation: factory-automation and motion-control peers — higher
  multiples for secular growth exposure.
- Building Products: HVAC and building-envelope peers — cyclical, lower
  multiples.
- Specialty Chemicals: coatings and adhesives peers — mature, low
  multiples.

Each segment uses its own relevant pure-play multiple range, per the
scenario constraint; no cross-segment averaging.

## 2. Corporate Cost Capitalization

Unallocated corporate overhead of $85M is a drag on consolidated
EBITDA. Capitalized at the segment-EBITDA-weighted average multiple:

```
Weighted avg multiple = (440×16 + 300×19 + 135×12 + 90×10) / 965
                     ≈ 15.8x (at segment midpoints)
```

Corporate drag on EV ≈ $85M × 15.8 = **$1,343M** deduction (midpoint).
Low/high deduction = $85M × 13.8x and $85M × 17.8x = $1,173M / $1,513M.

## 4. Conglomerate Discount vs Trading EV

Illustrative current trading EV (SMB) = $11,500M.

```
Discount = (SOTP_mid − Trading_EV) / SOTP_mid
        = ($13,917 − $11,500) / $13,917
        ≈ 17.4%
```

17% sits inside the 10-25% empirical conglomerate-discount band for
diversified industrials; activist claim of a meaningful gap is
consistent with the SOTP arithmetic, but this does not prove the gap
will close without a catalyst (spin, sale, buyback, capital return).

## 6. Sensitivity — ±1 Turn on Each Segment's Multiple

Starting from midpoint SOTP EV of $13,917M, moving each segment's
multiple by ±1x:

| Segment | EBITDA | EV swing per 1x | % of total SOTP |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Aerospace & Defense | 440 | ±440 | 3.2% |
| Industrial Automation | 300 | ±300 | 2.2% |
| Building Products | 135 | ±135 | 1.0% |
| Specialty Chemicals | 90 | ±90 | 0.7% |

Aerospace drives the largest absolute swing; its multiple selection is
the most impactful call in the analysis. Specialty Chemicals is the
least sensitive — spinning or selling it would de-clutter the story
but does not move SOTP value much.

## Summary

- Segment-level valuation suggests the company trades at roughly a
  17% conglomerate discount to its midpoint SOTP EV, within the
  historical 10-25% band.
- The SOTP value is a theoretical ceiling; realizing it requires a
  catalyst (spin, sale, or capital return), not just disclosure.
- Aerospace is the most valuable and the most sensitive segment;
  Specialty Chemicals is the smallest lever.
- Corporate overhead of $85M is not free; treating it as zero would
  overstate SOTP by ~$1.3B at segment-weighted multiples.
- The activist's proposal (spin Aerospace, sell Chemicals) is logically
  consistent with the SOTP view, but the board should weigh execution
  risk and dis-synergies against the potential value-gap closure.
