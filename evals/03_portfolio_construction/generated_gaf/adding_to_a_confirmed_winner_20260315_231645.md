

# Portfolio Construction Memo: Position Sizing Review — Enterprise Software Name

## Executive Summary

**Position**: Enterprise Software Co. (Long)
**Current Weight**: 4.4% NAV (up from 2.0% initiation, entirely via appreciation)
**Analyst Request**: Actively add to 6.0%
**Recommendation**: **DENY the add. Trim to 3.5–4.0%. Revisit active add post-LTCG qualification.**

**One-sentence thesis**: The position has already been "added to" by the market — passive drift has oversized it 2.2x beyond original intent, risk contribution has ballooned 2.7x, the majority of recent returns are factor-driven (not alpha), and adding to 6% would breach the 5% single-name concentration limit. The correct action is disciplined trimming, not doubling down on a momentum-loaded winner.

## I. Passive Drift vs. Active Sizing: The Position Already Sized Itself

| Metric | At Initiation (9mo ago) | Current State | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Portfolio weight | 2.0% | 4.4% | +2.2x |
| Risk contribution | 1.8% of portfolio risk | 4.8% of portfolio risk | +2.7x |
| Conviction score | 6/10 | 8/10 | +1.3x |
| Momentum factor loading | 0.15 | 0.72 | +4.8x |
| Position rank in long book | Mid-tier | **#1 largest** | — |

**The core problem**: Weight grew 2.2x but conviction only grew 1.3x. Risk contribution grew 2.7x. The position is already dramatically oversized relative to the conviction upgrade. At 6/10 conviction, a 2% weight implied ~0.33% weight per conviction point. At 8/10 conviction, a proportional sizing would be **2.67%** — well below the current 4.4%, let alone the requested 6.0%.

The analyst is conflating "the stock went up a lot" with "we should own more." These are different statements. Appreciation-driven drift is not an active sizing decision — it's an accident of portfolio mechanics. Every basis point above the original 2% allocation was never underwritten by a sizing committee. The position became the largest in the long book without anyone deciding it should be.

**Framework**: Any position that drifts >50% above its target weight through appreciation should trigger a mandatory review. This position drifted 120% above target. That alone demands a trim discussion, not an add discussion.

## II. Factor Attribution: You're Buying Momentum, Not Alpha

| Return Component | Contribution (pp) | Share of Total |
|---|---|---|
| **Alpha (idiosyncratic)** | 32pp | 41% |
| **Momentum factor** | ~34pp (est.) | ~44% |
| **Other systematic factors** | ~12pp (est.) | ~15% |
| **Total return** | 78pp (recent period) | 100% |

Source: context.factor_attribution — 46pp of 78pp recent return is factor-driven; only 32pp is alpha.

**This is the most important data point in the analysis.** Nearly 60% of recent performance came from factor exposure, not stock-specific alpha. The momentum loading surged from 0.15 to 0.72 — a near-5x increase that transforms the risk character of this position.

### Why this matters for the add decision:

1. **Adding at 0.72 momentum loading means buying momentum factor exposure at elevated levels.** You're not adding to an alpha-generating idea — you're increasing a momentum bet at a time when the factor is already richly expressed in the position.

2. **Momentum factor reversals are non-linear.** Historical momentum crashes (2009, 2020, sector rotations) produce drawdowns of 20–40% in weeks. A 6% position with 0.72 momentum loading would contribute outsized portfolio damage in a reversal.

3. **The alpha component alone justifies ~1.8% weight, not 6%.** If you strip out factor returns and size purely on alpha generation, the position should be smaller than it is today, not larger.

4. **"Thesis confirmed" ≠ "alpha confirmed."** The stock may have gone up for reasons that have nothing to do with the analyst's thesis. Enterprise software as a sector has benefited from AI narrative tailwinds (per Source 8: AI mentions in 80% of Fortune 500 earnings calls) and broad momentum flows. The analyst must demonstrate that the 32pp of alpha is attributable to their specific thesis, not sector beta mislabeled as alpha.

## III. Risk Contribution Drift: Unintentional Risk Budget Allocation

| Risk Metric | Original Intent | Current Reality | Proposed (6%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weight | 2.0% | 4.4% | 6.0% |
| Risk contribution | 1.8% | 4.8% | ~7.0% (est.) |
| Share of total risk budget | Low | **Largest single-name** | Dominant |
| Concentration limit | 5% max | 4.4% (within) | **6.0% — BREACH** |

The risk contribution grew 2.7x while the weight grew 2.2x. This divergence means the position's correlation to the rest of the portfolio increased — it's not just bigger, it's more correlated. Adding to 6% would likely push risk contribution to ~7% of portfolio risk, meaning a single enterprise software name would drive roughly 1/14th of total portfolio variance.

**Concentration limit breach**: The 5% single-name limit exists for a reason. Adding to 6% is a policy violation, full stop. Even if the PM has discretion to grant exceptions, doing so for a momentum-loaded position at all-time highs is precisely the scenario the limit was designed to prevent. Concentration limits are most valuable when they're hardest to follow.

**Risk budget question**: Did anyone allocate 4.8% of the risk budget to this name? No. It happened passively. The portfolio construction function must ask: if we were building the portfolio from scratch today, would we allocate 4.8% of risk (let alone 7%) to a single enterprise software name with 0.72 momentum loading? Almost certainly not.

## IV. Tax Lot Management: Timing Creates a Clear Decision Framework

| Tax Consideration | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unrealized gain | $12M |
| Time to LTCG qualification | 3 months |
| STCG rate (est.) | ~37% (ordinary income) |
| LTCG rate (est.) | ~20% |
| Tax cost of selling now | ~$4.4M |
| Tax cost of selling in 3 months | ~$2.4M |
| **Tax savings from waiting** | **~$2.0M** |

This creates a natural decision tree:

1. **Do not add new capital now.** Adding $8M+ in new purchases creates a fresh tax lot that restarts the LTCG clock, complicating future trim decisions and reducing portfolio flexibility.

2. **Do not aggressively trim now** (unless risk limits demand it). The $2M tax savings from waiting 3 months is material. A modest trim to stay within concentration limits is defensible; a full rebalance to target weight should wait.

3. **Set a calendar trigger at LTCG qualification date.** At that point, reassess the full position with fresh factor attribution and rebalance to appropriate weight with favorable tax treatment.

**Exception**: If the position breaches 5% through continued appreciation before LTCG qualification, a defensive trim is warranted regardless of tax consequences. Risk management supersedes tax optimization.

## VI. Addressing the "Thesis Confirmed" Argument

The analyst's conviction increase from 6/10 to 8/10 is noted and respected. But "thesis confirmed" is a necessary condition for holding, not a sufficient condition for adding. Several rebuttals:

1. **Price already reflects the thesis.** A 120% appreciation means the market has likely priced in much of what the analyst sees. The question isn't "is the thesis right?" but "is there incremental upside from here that justifies incremental risk?"

2. **Conviction ≠ sizing.** An 8/10 conviction score at current prices implies a different position size than 8/10 conviction at entry prices. The expected return from here is mechanically lower (unless the analyst has raised their target proportionally, which should be scrutinized).

3. **Confirmation bias risk is highest when positions are working.** The analyst's conviction increase may be partly driven by the stock's performance validating their ego, not new fundamental information. The PM should probe: what specific new information drove conviction from 6 to 8? Would that information alone justify a 6% position if we were initiating today?

4. **The right question is not "should we add?" but "would we build this position today?"** If starting fresh with 8/10 conviction, would the sizing committee approve a 6% position in a single enterprise software name with 0.72 momentum loading, 59% factor-driven returns, and a stock at all-time highs? The answer is almost certainly no.
