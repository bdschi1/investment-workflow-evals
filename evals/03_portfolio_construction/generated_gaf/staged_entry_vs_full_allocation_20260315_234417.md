The analyst's $18M order is **0.72% of daily volume**. At $2.5B ADV, this executes in the first 5 minutes of the trading day with negligible footprint.

Anyone citing market impact as the rationale for staging this name is confusing a $120B liquid large-cap with a $2B small-cap. The staging decision here is purely about **price risk and catalyst timing**, not execution quality.

## The Rally Problem: 14.5% in a Month, RSI 72

This is the one legitimate reason to hesitate. Let's be honest about what the data says:

**Bear case for immediate full deployment:**
- RSI 72 = technically overbought; mean-reversion probability elevated
- 14.5% in 30 days = ~2.5 standard deviations above normal monthly return for large-cap tech
- Pre-event hype rallies frequently give back 30-50% of gains before the event
- Buying the last 8.2% of a hype rally is a classic behavioral trap

**Bull case against waiting:**
- RSI 72 is overbought, not extreme (>80 would be more concerning)
- Pre-event drift is well-documented — stocks with legitimate catalysts often grind higher into the event
- Waiting for a pullback that doesn't come means entering at even higher prices or missing the event entirely
- The 14.5% rally may reflect genuine information leakage / smart money positioning, not just hype

### The Critical Question

Is the 14.5% rally **hype** or **information**?

- If hype → stock likely pulls back 4-6% before the event → staging or waiting adds value
- If information → stock continues drifting higher → staging costs money
- **We can't know** — but at 8/10 conviction, the base case is that the thesis is correct, which means the rally is at least partially informational

### What History Says About Pre-Event Overbought Signals

Large-cap tech names with RSI 70-75 heading into known catalysts within 6 weeks:
- ~40% pull back 3-5% before the event
- ~35% consolidate sideways
- ~25% continue higher

This means there's a **~40% chance** a tactical entry 3-5% lower is available, but a **60% chance** waiting costs money. The expected value favors deploying now with a modest tactical reserve.

- Only sacrifices 2 weeks of exposure on 1/3 of the position, not 3 weeks

**vs. 3-week staging at $6M/week:**
- Captures 67% of the position immediately vs. 33%
- Hard deadline at T+14 ensures full deployment 4 weeks before catalyst (vs. 3 weeks under the PM's plan)
- Staging $6M/week is a liquidity-driven cadence applied to a name where liquidity is irrelevant

**vs. Waiting for a pullback:**
- 60% probability the pullback doesn't come before the catalyst
- Waiting is a tactical bet against a strategic thesis — if conviction is 8/10, the default should be deployment
- "Waiting for a better entry" on a time-bound catalyst is how you miss the trade entirely

## Key Principles for the Staging Decision

1. **Staging is a tool, not a default.** The question is always "what problem does staging solve here?" If the answer is "it feels more conservative," that's not a reason.

2. **Market impact is the original reason staging exists.** When impact is trivial (<5 bps), staging's primary benefit disappears. Any remaining benefit is purely about price risk management.

3. **Catalyst timing dominates.** A time-bound thesis changes the calculus entirely. Staging into a catalyst means accepting that a portion of your position will have reduced exposure to the very event that justifies the trade.

4. **Conviction level matters.** Staging makes sense when you're building conviction (6/10 → 8/10 over time). When conviction is already 8/10, staging is a conviction tax — you're paying for optionality you don't need.

5. **Price action is a legitimate input, not a veto.** An overbought signal on a high-conviction catalyst trade warrants a tactical concession (reserve a portion for a pullback), not a strategic retreat (3-week drip).

6. **Always set a hard deployment deadline.** If you reserve capital for a pullback, define when you deploy regardless. Open-ended "waiting for a better price" is how positions never get built.
